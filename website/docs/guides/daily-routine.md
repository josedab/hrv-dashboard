# Daily Routine

Consistent, properly-timed measurements are the foundation of reliable HRV tracking. This guide covers best practices for building a sustainable morning readiness routine.

## Measurement Timing

For the most accurate and consistent readings:

- **Measure within 5 minutes of waking** — Your nervous system is in its most stable state immediately upon waking. Measurements taken later in the day are affected by accumulated stress, caffeine, food, and activity.
- **Before caffeine or exercise** — Both stimulate your sympathetic nervous system and skew readings upward. Wait until *after* you've logged your reading to have your coffee or start your workout.
- **In a seated or supine position** — Posture affects heart rate variability. Find a comfortable, consistent position and stick with it. Supine (lying down) is ideal but not required if sitting works for you.

## Why Consistency Matters More Than Perfection

You don't need a perfect reading every single day. Instead, focus on:

- **Same time each morning** — Your body's rhythms respond to routine. Measuring at 6:15 AM every day is better than measuring at 5:30 AM one day and 7:45 AM the next.
- **Same conditions** — Measure in the same location, same position, ideally before similar pre-measurement activities (no water, no bathroom break, etc.). The goal is to minimize variables.
- **Building a streak** — Even a single missed day resets your consecutive day count, but the data still matters. One missed day doesn't invalidate the trend.

A "good" baseline is built over weeks, not days. Small variations are normal. Trust the trend.

## Acting on Your Verdict

The three verdicts tell you how to approach your training today:

### **Go Hard** (rMSSD ≥ 95th percentile of your baseline)
- Your nervous system is recovered and primed.
- **Action**: Push intensity. This is the day for your hardest interval sessions, competition, or maximum effort work.
- You're in a sympathetic-ready state — use it wisely.

### **Moderate** (rMSSD 80th–95th percentile)
- You're capable and recovered enough to train.
- **Action**: Train normally, but avoid maximum effort or competition. Save the hardest workouts for "Go Hard" days.
- Maintain fitness with structured but controlled work.

### **Rest** (rMSSD < 80th percentile)
- Your nervous system is fatigued or recovering.
- **Action**: Prioritize recovery — easy walking, yoga, mobility work, meditation. If you must train, keep it light and aerobic.
- Push here and you'll dig yourself deeper. One recovery day now saves several later.

## Understanding Your Streak

The streak counter shows how many *consecutive days* you've logged a measurement:

- **Streak continues**: You measure every single day.
- **Streak resets**: Miss a day, and the counter goes back to 1.
- **Why it matters**: A long streak is motivating *and* a sign of data consistency. The longer your streak, the more reliable your baseline and verdicts.

If you miss a day, don't stress — just pick up where you left off tomorrow. The data you've collected is still valid.

## Subjective Logging

After each HRV measurement, you're prompted to log subjective details:

- **Perceived readiness (1–5)**: How do you *feel* this morning? Often correlates with HRV, but not always. Sometimes you feel great despite moderate HRV (or vice versa). Over time, this feedback loop improves your intuition.
- **Training type**: Strength, endurance, sport, rest day, etc. Helps you spot patterns (e.g., "my HRV always dips after hard interval sessions").
- **Sleep hours & quality**: Track how many hours you slept and rate the quality (1–5). Poor sleep consistently predicts lower HRV.
- **Stress level (1–5)**: Mental/emotional stress is as real as physical stress. High stress will suppress HRV.
- **Notes**: Any context (e.g., "stayed up late," "got sick," "won a big game," "traveled to new timezone"). These notes help explain outliers.

Over time, subjective logs reveal *your* patterns. Some people's HRV is highly stress-sensitive; others recover quickly from hard training. The app is a personal biofeedback tool — use the logs to learn *your* physiology.
