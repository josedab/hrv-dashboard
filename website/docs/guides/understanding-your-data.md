# Understanding Your Data

This guide explains what each screen and metric in the app shows you, and how to interpret the data to make decisions about your training and recovery.

## Home Screen

The home screen gives you today's snapshot:

- **Today's Verdict** ("Go Hard," "Moderate," or "Rest") — Displayed prominently at the top. This is your primary decision tool for the day.
- **rMSSD Value** — Your raw heart rate variability metric for today's measurement, displayed with 2 decimal places.
- **Sparkline Trend** — A visual graph showing your recent rMSSD values (typically the last 7–14 days, depending on your baseline window). The sparkline includes a baseline overlay (a reference line) so you can quickly see whether you're above or below your personal baseline.
- **Stats Summary** — Quick glance at your mean heart rate, key HRV metrics (SDNN, pNN50), and artifact rate from today's measurement.

The sparkline is your best friend for spotting patterns at a glance.

## History Screen

The history screen shows a chronological list of all your past measurements:

- **Trend header** — At the top, a small sparkline showing your overall trend over the selected time period (7 days, 30 days, all-time, etc.).
- **Session list** — Each row represents one measurement session, showing the date, verdict, rMSSD value, and a quick summary of subjective data (sleep hours, stress level, etc.).
- **Scrolling** — Scroll backward in time to review past sessions. Older sessions appear at the bottom.

## Session Detail View

Tap any session in the history list to open the detailed view:

- **Full metrics** — All recorded HRV metrics at 2 decimal places for rMSSD and SDNN, 1 decimal place for mean HR and pNN50, and 4 decimal places for artifact rate.
- **RR plot** — A scatter plot showing the distribution of RR intervals (the time between heartbeats) during your measurement. This helps visualize the "spread" of your intervals — a wider spread indicates higher variability and better recovery.
- **Subjective log** — Displays the notes you entered after the measurement: perceived readiness, training type, sleep data, stress level, and any written notes.

This view is useful for understanding *why* a particular day was high or low. Review it when you're curious about a specific session or trying to spot patterns.

## Interpreting Trends

### Improving HRV Over Time

If you see your sparkline trending upward over weeks:
- Your fitness and recovery capacity are improving.
- Your baseline is likely stable and trustworthy.
- Training stimulus + recovery = adaptation. Keep it up.

### Sudden Drops

A sudden dip in HRV (below your baseline) can signal:
- **Recent hard training** — Especially interval work or strength. 1–2 days of lower HRV is normal recovery.
- **Poor sleep** — One night of bad sleep often shows up as lower HRV the next morning.
- **Illness or infection** — Particularly viral; HRV often drops before symptoms appear.
- **Mental stress** — Work deadlines, relationship issues, travel, etc.
- **Alcohol or late-night eating** — Both suppress HRV the following morning.

If the dip lasts 3+ days, it's time to prioritize recovery.

### Gradual Recovery

After a hard training block or stressor, you might see HRV gradually climb back over several days. This is normal and expected. Don't panic if it takes a week or two to return to baseline — that's adaptation.

## When NOT to Worry

A single low reading is rarely a problem. Context matters:

- **One-off low readings after poor sleep** — Sleep is the #1 driver of HRV. One bad night doesn't reflect your fitness or recovery capacity. But if poor sleep becomes chronic, it will.
- **After hard training** — Expect dips for 1–2 days after intense workouts. This is *not* a sign of overtraining; it's a sign that you pushed hard.
- **Alcohol the night before** — Alcohol disrupts sleep quality and suppresses HRV. One night of drinking often shows up as low HRV the next morning.
- **During illness** — Colds and flu typically lower HRV. Rest and recover; you'll rebound quickly once you're healthy.

**The signal emerges over time.** One low reading is noise. Three consecutive low readings is data. Use the sparkline and history to spot *patterns*, not to obsess over individual data points.
